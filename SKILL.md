# Name-It: Structured Naming in One Session

A systematic method for generating, evaluating, and selecting names. Based on the principle that naming is positioning — you're not labeling a thing, you're compressing its story, feeling, and promise into a word.

## Core Philosophy

A name does four jobs simultaneously:
1. **Personifies** — sets tone and character (Apple = friendly; Microsoft = serious)
2. **Encompasses** — triggers the full experience in the listener's mind (Uber = app + black cars + convenience)
3. **Rallies** — serves as internal banner and external billboard; great names produce demonyms (Googlers, Amazonians)
4. **Spreads** — enables word-of-mouth ("Google it"); if it resonates, marketing is half done

## Process: 6 Phases

Work through these in order. Adapt depth to the user's time and context.

---

### Phase 1: Orientation

Before generating a single name, align on identity. Ask the user these questions (skip any they've already answered):

- **What are you building?** (be concrete — not vision, but what it *does*)
- **What breaks if you don't exist?** (this is your purpose — specific enough to exclude half the market)
- **What happens in the first 5 minutes someone uses it?** (the promise — must survive contact with reality)
- **Who is your most important audience?** (primary only — and what other brands do they love?)
- **Pick ONE dominant feeling:** Relief / Momentum / High-Status / Wonder / Rebellion / Belonging / Control / other
- **Tonal position on three spectrums:**
  - Playful ↔ Serious
  - Accessible ↔ Exclusive
  - Innovative ↔ Traditional
- **Non-starters:** Words, vibes, or associations that are forbidden (competitor-adjacent, cultural taboos, hard-to-spell-over-phone, pharma-sounding, etc.)
- **Competitor landscape:** What are competitors called? Where do they cluster? Where is the open territory?

Capture answers concisely. These become your compass for every later decision.

---

### Phase 2: Exploration

Goal: generate 50+ raw candidates (more is better — great names come from volume, not polish). Use multiple angles:

**2a. Free Naming** — Just write names. Any names. Get obvious ideas out. No judgment.

**2b. Springboards** — Take purpose/promise/feeling words and escalate them:
- Word → broader theme → specific reference → candidate
- Example: Calm → Sleep → Night Sky → Orion
- Example: Elegant → French refinement → Câlin

**2c. Meaningful Facets** — Deep-dive associations with the category, product history, founding story, locations, books, characters, historical figures. Work backwards from great names for inspiration:
- A24 = Italian highway where founder had the idea
- Stripe = magnetic stripe on credit cards
- Palantir = seeing stone from Lord of the Rings

**2d. Lexical Rabbitholing** — Follow daisy-chains through Wikipedia, obscure dictionaries, etymology. Go beyond Greek gods and startup tropes.

**2e. Modification & Hacks** — Creative misspellings, portmanteaus, dropped vowels, added letters, descriptive suffixes (Labs, Studio, HQ, etc.)

**Name type palette** (use as generation prompts — aim to cover multiple categories):

| Type | What it is | Examples |
|------|-----------|----------|
| Descriptive | Says what it does | Dropbox, YouTube, LinkedIn |
| Compound | Links two ideas | AirPods, WhatsApp, PayPal |
| Experiential | Conveys the feeling/outcome | Slack, Zoom, Tinder |
| Evocative | Familiar word in new context | Apple, Stripe, Nest, Amazon |
| Metaphor | Borrows meaning by association | Robinhood, Lemonade, Safari |
| Invented | Coined/derived new word | Google, Spotify, Kodak |
| Spelling Play | Misspell/modify for ownership | Lyft, Flickr, Canva |
| Identity/Place | Rooted in person, place, heritage | Patagonia, Tesla, Cisco |
| Attitude/Edge | Bold stance or personality | Virgin, Liquid Death, Vice |
| Special Wrongness | Unexpected pairing; 1+1=3 | Teenage Engineering, Impossible Foods, Browser Company of New York |

Present candidates organized by type. Tag each with a brief rationale connecting it to the orientation.

---

### Phase 3: Distillation

Cut ruthlessly. Evaluate every candidate on six dimensions:

| Dimension | Key Question |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Depth** | Layers of meaning? Metaphorical weight without being heavy? |
| **Temperature** | Warm/inviting or cold/distant? Match the intended feeling? |
| **Voice** | Sound aloud — crisp? Flowing? Friction? |
| **Visual** | Looks good in ALL CAPS, lowercase, tiny screens, billboards? |
| **Differentiation** | Stands out from competitors? Trademarkable? |
| **Special Wrongness** | Unforgettable newness? Would it start a trend? |

Then score finalists on three axes:

| Axis | Test |
|------|------|
| **Memory** | Will someone remember it after one encounter? Can they spell it without asking? |
| **Meaning** | Does it connect emotionally, not just literally? Are you excited to say it daily? |
| **Range** | Can it grow with the company? Does it trap you in one category? |

2 of 3 axes = viable. 3 of 3 = rare and magical. 0-1 of 3 = cut.

**Reality checks before advancing:**
- Legal scan: Quick USPTO/TMView search for identical marks in your class
- Language check: Say it 10x aloud, check slang dictionaries, translate into key languages
- Landscape check: Plot on competitor map — does it camouflage or differentiate?

Target **12–15 viable names** after this phase.

---

### Phase 4: Validation

Test with people who don't care about your feelings.

**8-Second Test:** Show name + one-line description for 8 seconds. Ask: "What do you expect from a company named ___?" Record exact first sentence. Then ask them to recall all names — note which stick.

**Phonetics Fast-Pass:**
- Phone test: Can someone spell it back after hearing it once?
- Open vs. closed ending: vowel-end = lighter (Carta), hard-stop = punchy (Ramp)
- Look-alikes to avoid: l/1/I, O/0, rn/m, vv/w

**Deeper Diligence (for top 5):**
- Trademark: Filter by class on TMView; distinctive names (suggestive/arbitrary/invented) are far easier to protect
- Domain: Don't sacrifice seed capital for .com purity. Modifiers (get-, try-, use-) and alt TLDs (.net, .ai, .dev, .io) are fine. Square ran on squareup.com for years.
- Cultural: Native speaker check in key markets for pronunciation and unintended meanings
- Mockups: Drop into headlines, app icons, social handles, multiple fonts. ALL CAPS, lowercase, mixed.
- Future-proofing: Pair with future product names. Does it produce a natural demonym?

Exit with **3–5 finalists** that passed each test.

---

### Phase 5: Commitment

One person decides. Committees find the safe middle; you want memorable.

**Decision methods:**
- Set each finalist as phone background for 3-4 days — constant low-pressure exposure reveals the winner
- Or: 45-min time-boxed meeting, someone argues for/against each, secret ballot, CEO holds final veto

**Lock-in (same day):**
1. File trademark application
2. Purchase/broker domains
3. Secure social handles
4. Draft internal name story

**Handling doubt:** Post-decision regret is universal and predictable. Remember: you ran a process; no name is perfect (Apple was "too simple," Google was "too silly"); every alternative had flaws you've forgotten; the brand you build around it is what makes it powerful.

---

### Phase 6: Stewardship

The name is 20% of the journey. The other 80% is consistent use, clear storytelling, and protection.

- Craft a founding myth (how the name came to be)
- Use it consistently everywhere — don't hedge with apologetic intros
- Build product names as a family, not random words
- Protect the trademark actively
- Great names deepen with repetition — Nike means victory through decades of use, not just etymology

---

## Interaction Guidelines

When helping a user name something:

1. **Start with Orientation questions.** Don't generate names until you understand purpose, promise, audience, feeling, and constraints. Even a quick version of this transforms results.
2. **Generate in volume.** Aim for 30-50+ candidates across multiple name types. Present in organized batches with brief rationales.
3. **Be honest in evaluation.** Use the six dimensions and three axes. Don't be precious — cut hard.
4. **Present finalists as a comparison.** Show Memory/Meaning/Range scores. Note tradeoffs clearly.
5. **Don't over-explain.** When presenting names, let them breathe. A name + one sentence of rationale is usually enough.
6. **Match the user's pace.** Some want the full afternoon process. Some want 10 options in 2 minutes. Adapt, but always ground in orientation first — even 3 quick questions dramatically improve output.
